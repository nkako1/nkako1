The brief

In April, a lady named Terra LaRock reached out to me. She is a Colorado-based psychologist, blogger, influencer and mother of two. She had started the Mindful Mamas Club – a place for self-care and mindfulness for moms. She was considering building an app to house meditations, mantras, and mindfulness exercises.

We spoke a bit about her goals for the app and I started playing with a proof of concept.

Visual design

My aim for the visual design was breezy, comforting, calming. I used two nested Enso symbols as the branding to signify the nested nature of mother and child.

Product design

Terra's approach to mindfulness is based on four pillars: guided meditations, "minipauses," breathing, and mantras. I structured the app so that a stressed-out mom could access relief as quickly as possible, but also so that browsing would be a calming and enjoyable experience.

the result

Although we didn't end up working together, Terra loved the POC: "First off, I was completely blown away with your vision for MMC. You brought tears to my eyes and it will forever be a pivotal moment in my journey to birthing The Mindful Mamas Club." Overall it was a great design exercise and I enjoyed very much giving shape to Terra's ideas.

The Problem

Historically, clients would transfer data files to LiveRamp via SFTP, which would then be parsed and mapped manually by the operations team. These files contain identifiers (email addresses, postal addresses, etc.) that we must recognize and flag. This is so the system can match this row of data to an anonymized person, and so we are absolutely sure we are not sending through any sensitive or personally identifiable information.

The Challenge

How can we simplify the highly technically complex process of taking in, mapping, and parsing data into a self-service, user-driven task?

Requirements

Honestly, there were too many to list. Let’s just say my goal was to simplify, simplify, simplify.

Process

This process was especially fun because I could feel the design evolve every time I user tested:

GATHERING DATA

In addition to the PM’s list of requirements, I wanted qualitative data from the operations team about how users talk, think, and work around ingestion, and quantitative data from our database on common ingestion situations.

ESTABLISHING FLOWS

I drew up flow charts to map out a user’s journey through ingestion, showing me where users would be performing repetitive or overly technical tasks, and where decisions could be simplified.

ITERATE, ITERATE, ITERATE

After a first pass, I gathered feedback from the design team before going back to the drawing board. I repeated this process several more times, widening my feedback circle each time until I was showing the UI to clients and watching them step through the process.

Key Design Decisions

Limit Configurability

At every step, a user potentially has a dozen options for advanced configurations which are only used by a tiny percentage of our client base (the “power users”). For the majority of clients, only one or two options at each step were relevant. I pushed to simplify each step as much as possible, and obscure the advanced configurations behind “Advanced options” triggers.

Process Map

My first version was a linear workflow of 4 steps. But as I iterated, I came to realize this was an oversimplification that could cause confusion to the user, as each step could potentially spawn a sub-workflow depending on the user’s input. So I moved on to a “map” instead of a linear workflow, which gave the user better orientation and simplified each task.

Subgrouped Tasks

Once I realized the map was the way to go, I had 6-8 discrete steps in this workflow. It became clear to me that these steps should be grouped into three.

Lessons Learned

To Assume Makes a ...Sump Out of Me

For this project, it was easy to make assumptions based on what I already knew of our system. A couple times, I had to check myself and reset to a user’s state of mind. I developed some fun methods for doing this, including physically relocating away from the engineering part of the office, talking with a friend in Sales for 5 minutes, or reading a few client emails. I developed a pretty good sense of when I was getting too in the weeds and practiced returning to a user’s state of mind with and without help. As someone who likes to dive into technical problems, this resetting practice will be very useful for me going forward.
