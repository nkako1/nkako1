A notifications framework for LiveRamp

The Problem

Here’s how it works: clients send their data to LiveRamp, which is sanitized and matched before being sent out to the client’s end platform. Until recently, the only way for a client to know where their data was in the process and how close it was to being delivered was to sign into our platform Connect periodically and check to see if a status symbol in a table had changed.

Client feedback from personally conducted user interviews and filtered back from our operations team overwhelmingly reported that this current state was cumbersome, time consuming, and often inaccurate.

The Challenge

How can we keep users updated in a noninvasive, helpful way? How can we ensure the system that we build is reusable, scalable, and itself not a burden on our system?

Insights

I gathered requirements from clients via user interviews, analyzed quantitative data gathered by the otherations team, and met with product leads. My goal was to understand how users navigated the lack of transparency in the platform and what workarounds they employed to keep up to date with their data.

MULTIPLE MEDIUMS

Many clients log onto our platform as few times as once a month. We needed to be able to support email as well as in-app as a medium for notifications.

SCOPING DOWN

We serve 5 different client types and 3 levels of user permissions. Notifications had to be easily scoped to the user’s client type and permission level.

ACTION ITEMS

Our team estimated 20 hrs a week savings in operational time if users were automatically notified when 1) their file failed and required a reupload and 2) their file was successful and required the user to take next steps.

BE SENSITIVE

A certain tier of our users, including international clients who have certain cultural expectations around notification, need to be opted out to start.

Deliverables

Over the course of a quarter, the team delivered

  • A notification service that any full stack team could include and use in their project
  • A series of notification emails with consistent templated branding & messaging
  • A dropdown UI in Connect to monitor incoming notifications
  • A notification center where notifications can be seen and managed in full
  • A notification subscription page

How it works

A PM creates a new notification in our internal admin app and specifies the intended audience, the type of notification (error, status, etc.), and the content for the in-app and email notifications.

The PM’s team calls the notification service and passes back any variables that may be used in the notification content, e.g. the name of the file or the reason why it failed.

The PM enables the notification in our internal app, scopes it only to him/her and tests. This was part of the intended design: that a PM could enable a notification in production and test it in a real environment, but only he/she would be able to see it. This way, we could monitor real-life elements of the user experience before release, like how an email was displayed for certain stricter email clients or what kind of volume a user of a given persona would receive in a week.

That’s it! Once the notification is enabled and scoped to all users, every Connect user has the ability to subscribe to this new notification and choose to receive in-app and/or email communication.

Impact

84.4% retention rate since release

15-20 hours saved per week
(That's half the workload of an operations manager.)

Lessons Learned

The Customer Isn’t Always Right

Hear me out. Our operations team has very close relationships with our biggest clients, sometimes so much as to speak to them daily. Building the notifications system was part of a company-wide effort to move towards self-service and automation. But it’s not particularly fun being told you now have to do something that your account manager has always done for you. For this reason, a couple larger clients I spoke with couldn’t see the value of receiving notifications that alerted them to a poorly formatted file or to an action required for a workflow. However, internal research had yielded an estimate of 20 hrs saved a week across the operations team, so:

Cost of building in engineering hours + approx. hours of client onboarding*% of client base over a certain ACV
_________________________________________________________________
Time savings across operations team

Approaching it this way, I estimated we would pay back the hours in under two months. And so it was a go!

Hi, I'm Kodos

A React component toolkit for LiveRamp

The Problem

Back in startup days, LiveRamp’s product was purely a backend system that the operations team manipulated to service their clients. Soon after I joined, a new objective was announced: self-service SaSS. We would take our platform Connect, which was then one page and read-only, and transform it into a standalone, self-service UI through which a user could complete any task currently performed by their account team. Previously, any UI work had been done completely ad hoc: all architecture, organization, and styling was done at the engineer’s discretion.

The Challenges (just a few)

How can we build a reliable, consistent platform from the ground up extremely quickly? How can we establish best practices across the team and prevent guerilla development, resulting in a frankenstein monster UI?

Goals

Enable quick and easy UI development for the team

Avoid messiness or nonstandard UI

Centralize design and UX standards

Process

I teamed up with a full-stack engineer and together we mapped out a plan to create a reusable library of React components.

We divided and conquered. I set about determining what components to include based on Connect’s functionality at the time and what we were currently building. I also did research and incorporated ideas from other toolkits and style guides. My partner architected the project and did the heavy lifting by building most of the components. At this stage we also opened it up to the rest of the full-stack team, circulating the list of components to be built and requirements for each.

For each component, either built by my partner or submitted by the team, I would step in later to style each component, QA it, and add it to our display page. I created global variables for colors, shading, and hover interactions so they would easy to tweak later.

Result

Once our components were built and the display page was complete, we did a final round of documentation for the toolkit. My partner published the npm package and our team was able to use it right away!

Kodos, as it is called for its green tinge, has enabled full-stack development at LiveRamp to explode. Full-stack developers can now put together a new page in minutes by stacking toolkit components like Legos, when previously they had to create every input, button, and table from scratch.

Impact

Estimated hours saved: according to a colleague, immeasurable. But if he had to put a number on it, 5 hours a week per full-stack developer, so around 100 hours a week across the team.

Lessons Learned

Open Source is Not Always the Answer

When we built the toolkit, our contributing team was 5 engineers. We all sat in the same bank of desks, so communication was extremely easy. As our full-stack team grew, more and more was lost in translation and our best practices eroded. Our open-source model allowed engineers across the organization to contribute, driving up complexity and limiting the effectiveness of the components by making them too flexible. Now we have to commit time and resources towards simplifying a few key components. If we were to do it all over again, I would have more explicit best practices around what functionality should be added directly to a component (hover patterns, onclick events) and what should be abstracted as a utility (applying colors or allowing an input to accept and parse a comma-separated string).

Drop That Data

Designing an ingestion system for LiveRamp

The Problem

Historically clients would transfer data files to LiveRamp via SFTP, which would then be parsed and mapped manually by the operations team. These files contain identifiers (email addresses, postal addresses, etc.) that we must recognize and flag. This is so the system can match this row of data to an anonymized person and so we are absolutely sure we are not sending through any sensitive or personally identifiable information.

The Challenge

How can we simplify the multi-step, highly complex process of mapping data into a self-service task?

Requirements

Honestly, there were too many to list. Let’s just say my goal was to simplify, simplify, simplify.

Process

This process was especially fun because I could feel the design evolve every time I user tested, both internally and externally:

GATHERING DATA

In addition to the PM’s list of requirements, I wanted qualitative data from the operations team about how users talk, think, and work around ingestion, and quantitative data from our database on common ingestion situations. This allowed me to understand which cases to optimize for and which were uncommon or advanced.

ESTABLISHING FLOWS

I drew up flow charts to map out a user’s journey through ingestion, which tended to branch off at each decision point. These charts allowed me to see where users would be performing repetitive or overly technical tasks, and where decisions could be simplified.

ITERATE, ITERATE, ITERATE

After a first pass, I gathered feedback from the design team before going back to the drawing board. I repeated this process several more times, widening my feedback circle each time until I was showing the UI to clients and watching them step through the process. This iteration helped me see areas in my design that could be improved and ultimately led me to my final design.

Key Design Decisions

Limit Configurability

At every step, a user potentially has a dozen options for advanced configurations which are only used by a tiny percentage of our client base (the “power users”). For the majority of clients, only one or two options at each step were relevant. I pushed to simplify each step as much as possible, and obscure the advanced configurations behind “Advanced options” triggers.

Process Map

My first version was a linear workflow of 4 steps. But as I iterated, I came to realize this was an oversimplification that could cause confusion to the user, as each step could potentially spawn a sub-workflow depending on the user’s input. So I moved on to a “map” instead of a linear workflow, which gave the user better orientation and simplified each task.

Subgrouped Tasks

Once I realized the map was the way to go, I had 6-8 discrete steps in this workflow. It became clear to me that these steps should be grouped into three.

Impact

This UI is still in the process of being built, so check back for updates!

Lessons Learned

To Assume Makes a ...Sump Out of Me

For this project, it was easy to make assumptions based on what I already knew of our system. A couple times, I had to check myself and reset to a user’s state of mind. I developed some fun methods for doing this, including physically relocating away from the engineering part of the office, talking with a friend in Sales for 5 minutes, or reading a few client emails. I developed a pretty good sense of when I was getting too in the weeds and practiced returning to a user’s state of mind with and without help. As someone who likes to dive into technical problems, this resetting practice will be very useful for me going forward.
